Stage 1 · SCAN · Fire Island
CAPTAIN'S RULE
Scanning Is Not Browsing

Browsing is passive. You open Twitter, scroll LinkedIn, click whatever the algorithm serves. You feel productive. You're not.

Scanning is active. It starts with a question: What problem am I solving this week? Then you hunt. You filter. You reject 90% of what you find, not because it's bad, but because it's not yours.

The scanner doesn't ask "what's interesting?" The scanner asks "what's useful to me, right now?"

[ ILLUSTRATION: Passive Browsing (scattered arrows) vs Active Scanning (focused beam) ]
"The quality of your learning is determined by the quality of your questions, not the quantity of your sources."
◈ Captain's Rule: Never scan without a question. The question is the compass. Without it, you drift.

WANTED POSTER
The Bookmark Graveyard
⚠ WANTED: THE BOOKMARK GRAVEYARD

Symptoms: 500+ saved articles. Zero processed. "I'll read it later" is your most common thought. Your Pocket account is where articles go to die.

Root cause: No filter. No intention. You're collecting without a question to answer.

The fix: Before you save anything, say out loud: "I need this because ___." If you can't finish the sentence, don't save it.

"A bookmark is a promise you made to yourself that you never intended to keep."

The Bookmark Graveyard is not a memory problem. It's an intention problem. Every bookmarked article without a purpose is cognitive debt: it takes up mental space while delivering zero returns.

One question per session. Three sources maximum. If it doesn't answer your question, let it go. The sea is full of fish. You only need the ones that feed your crew.

The cure for the graveyard is on the next page.

Stage 1 · SCAN · Fire Island
TREASURE FOUND
How Darwin Scanned Everything

Charles Darwin didn't stumble upon natural selection. He hunted it.

For twenty years, he read across disciplines: geology, botany, animal husbandry, economics. But he wasn't browsing. He had one question: Why do species change?

When he read Malthus on population, he wasn't studying economics. He was scanning for a mechanism that explained variation. He found it: competition for limited resources.

"Darwin's genius wasn't in the answer. It was in the question that guided his scanning for two decades."

Every book he read, every specimen he collected, every letter he wrote was filtered through that single intention. He scanned everything, but he kept only what answered his question.

◈ The principle he stole: Scan with a question. Without one, you're just a tourist in the library.

🌍 Ownership
Stage 2 · TRANSLATE · Earth Continent
CAPTAIN'S RULE
Translation, Not Copying

Most people stop at scanning. They find something good and they file it. Highlight it. Screenshot it. "Saved for later."

Translation is the act of passing someone else's idea through the filter of your purpose. Not "this is interesting" but "this means X for my situation."

When you translate, you own the idea. It stops being theirs and starts being yours. This is the moment learning begins.

[ ILLUSTRATION: Raw Idea → Your Filter → Your Version ]
"An idea you can't rephrase in your own words is an idea you don't understand."
◈ Captain's Rule: Translation is not paraphrasing. It's connecting an external idea to an internal problem. No connection, no translation.

Stage 2 · TRANSLATE · Earth Continent
TREASURE FOUND
How Jobs Translated Calligraphy

Steve Jobs sat in on a calligraphy class at Reed College in 1972. He wasn't a calligraphy student. He wasn't even enrolled. He was auditing, scanning, looking for something he couldn't name.

He didn't copy the letterforms. He didn't become a calligrapher. He translated.

"He didn't steal the letters. He stole the love of letters."

What Jobs extracted was not serif vs. sans-serif. It was the principle that the shape of information affects how humans receive it. That beauty is not decoration, it's function.

Ten years later, designing the Macintosh, he translated the principle: if the shape of a letter affects feeling, then the shape of an interface affects usability. The Mac became the first personal computer with proportional fonts and kerning.

◈ The principle he stole: Beauty is not decorative. It's structural. And structure can be translated across any medium.

Stage 3 · EVALUATE · Water Delta
CAPTAIN'S RULE
The Courage to Reject

Most people can't throw things away. Ideas, articles, frameworks: they hoard them all. "It might be useful someday."

Evaluation is the act of judging. Not everything you translated is worth keeping. The evaluator asks three questions:

1. Is it true? (Evidence, not opinion)
2. Is it mine? (Fits my context, not just someone else's)
3. Is it now? (Relevant today, not "someday")

If the answer to any of these is no, discard it. Gracefully.

"Curation is not about what you include. It's about what you have the discipline to exclude."
◈ Captain's Rule: The quality of your knowledge is measured by what you reject, not what you collect.

Root cause: Fear of missing out. The belief that more inputs equal better outputs. They don't.

"You don't need more ideas. You need fewer, better ideas applied with more discipline."

The Everything Collector feels productive because the collection grows. But growth without pruning is a jungle, not a garden. The best thinkers are aggressive curators.

TREASURE FOUND
How Buffett Evaluates Ideas

Warren Buffett reads 500 pages a day. His desk has two trays: "In" and "Too Hard."

Most ideas go in "Too Hard." Not because they're bad, but because they're outside his circle of competence. He doesn't invest in technology because he doesn't understand it deeply enough.

"If I can't explain why this will work in one paragraph, I don't understand it well enough to bet on it."

This isn't laziness. It's the most disciplined evaluation framework in finance. Buffett's edge isn't intelligence. It's the courage to say "I don't know enough about this to have an opinion" and move on.

Most people can't resist having an opinion on everything. Buffett resists with the discipline of a master evaluator.

◈ The principle he stole: The best evaluators aren't the ones who accept the most. They're the ones who reject with confidence.

Stage 4 · ABSTRACT · Air Kingdom
CAPTAIN'S RULE
Extract the Principle, Not the Example

The example is what happened. The principle is why it happened.

When you read about Toyota's production system, the example is kanban boards and just-in-time delivery. The principle is: make problems visible immediately so they can be solved at the source.

The example is bound to Toyota, to manufacturing, to the 1960s. The principle is universal, timeless, transferable to software, healthcare, education, anything.

"Don't steal the kanban board. Steal the principle that made the kanban board work."
[ ILLUSTRATION: Example (Toyota) → Tactic (visibility) → Principle (surface problems fast) ]
◈ Captain's Rule: Abstraction is climbing the ladder from "what they did" to "why it works." The higher you climb, the more domains you can apply it to.

Stage 4 · ABSTRACT · Air Kingdom
TREASURE FOUND
How Musk Abstracts First Principles

When Elon Musk wanted to build rockets, the industry quoted him $65 million per launch. He didn't accept the price. He didn't negotiate. He abstracted.

"What is a rocket made of? Aluminum, titanium, copper, carbon fiber. What do those materials cost on the commodity market?" About 2% of the price of a finished rocket.

"The cost of a thing is not the cost of its components. The markup is in the assembly, the process, the assumptions."

Musk didn't copy anyone's rocket. He abstracted the cost structure down to physics and materials, then rebuilt from first principles. SpaceX now launches at a fraction of the industry average.

◈ The principle he stole: First-principles thinking is abstraction applied to reality. Strip away assumptions until you hit bedrock truth.

Stage 5 · LEVERAGE · Aether Nexus
CAPTAIN'S RULE
Knowledge Without Action Is Decoration

You scanned with intention. You translated through your purpose. You evaluated with courage. You abstracted the principle. Now what?

If the answer is "I'll think about it," you've failed. The entire STEAL cycle exists to produce one thing: a change in your behavior.

Leverage means applying the abstracted principle to a specific situation, this week, in a way that is observable and reversible. Not "someday." Tomorrow.

"The question isn't 'did I learn something?' The question is 'did I do something different because of what I learned?'"
◈ Captain's Rule: If you can't name what you'll do differently tomorrow, the STEAL cycle is incomplete. Go back to Translate.

Stage 5 · LEVERAGE · Aether Nexus
TREASURE FOUND
How Kleon Leveraged Stealing

Austin Kleon read voraciously. Art books, poetry, business thinking, music theory. But he didn't just read. He had a practice: newspaper blackout poems.

He'd take a newspaper, black out most of the words with marker, and leave a poem visible in the remaining words. He wasn't creating from nothing. He was stealing structure from journalism and remixing it into art.

"That practice became a book. The book became a movement. 'Steal Like an Artist' sold over a million copies."

Kleon didn't just write about stealing. He leveraged the principle every day, in a specific practice, with an observable output. The newspaper didn't know it was being stolen from. The poem didn't care where it came from.

◈ The principle he stole: Leverage is a daily practice, not a one-time event. Small, consistent applications compound into transformation.
